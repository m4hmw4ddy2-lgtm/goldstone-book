COMMUTATION OF GOULDSTONE'S SENTENCE.
Liverpool Daily Post, 1 October 1883

The Home Secretary has requested two physicians to examine the convict Gouldstone, and report as to his mental condition. The British Medical Journal says that the Home Secretary intends to commute the sentence on Gouldstone to penal servitude for life, "the evidence of insanity being sufficient to justify a modification of the sentence." We venture to say that such a termination of the case would be absurd. If there is sufficient evidence of insanity to justify a commutation of the sentence, it would follow as a matter of course that Gouldstone would be sent, not into penal servitude, but to a criminal lunatic asylum, where he would be properly cared for. He is either a murderer of the worst type or an irresponsible lunatic.

This is an EDITORIAL response that directly anticipates and corrects the BMJ's own speculation -- correctly arguing that simple "penal servitude for life" would be the wrong outcome if insanity were truly established, and that committal to a criminal lunatic asylum (i.e. Broadmoor) would be the logically consistent result. This newspaper's editorial reasoning was, in the event, vindicated: William was NOT sent to ordinary penal servitude but specifically to Broadmoor, exactly as this piece argues should happen "as a matter of course."
KEY PHRASE: "He is either a murderer of the worst type or an irresponsible lunatic." A clean, stark formulation of the binary choice the case posed to contemporary opinion -- useful framing for Part Three.
