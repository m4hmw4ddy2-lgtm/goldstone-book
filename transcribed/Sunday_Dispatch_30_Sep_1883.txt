THE CASE OF WILLIAM GOULDSTONE.
Sunday Dispatch, 30 September 1883

The Home Secretary has addressed a letter to the Sheriff of Essex, Mr. J. Oxley Parker, signifying the Queen's commands that the sentence of death passed upon William Gouldstone for the murder of his five children at Walthamstow, be respited until Monday, the 8th of October, in order that inquiry may be made as to the prisoner's sanity. The execution was fixed for Monday next.

At the request of the Home Secretary, Dr. Clarke, of London, and Dr. Orange, of the Criminal Lunatic Asylum at Broadmoor, have visited Chelmsford Prison to examine the convict William Gouldstone, lying under sentence of death for the murder of his five children at Walthamstow, with a view to reporting upon his state of mind. Dr. Clarke and Dr. Orange, who spent six or seven hours with the convict, will make their report direct to Sir William Harcourt. During the week Gouldstone has been visited by several of his relatives. He pays diligent attention to the ministrations of the prison chaplain, the Rev. W. Faithfull Lumley.

The British Medical Journal says: -- "We are informed that the Home Secretary intends to commute the sentence on the convict Gouldstone to penal servitude for life, the evidence of insanity being sufficient to justify a modification of the sentence."

TRANSCRIPTION NOTES
Source: Sunday Dispatch, 30 September 1883

KEY MAJOR DETAIL CONFIRMED: TWO doctors, not one, examined William at Chelmsford -- Dr Orange of Broadmoor AND a previously unidentified "Dr Clarke, of London." (NOTE: not to be confused with Emma/Charles Clarke the landlords -- this is a different person, a London physician.) This corrects/expands the picture from the BMJ 29 September piece, which speculated that "Dr Orange... and Dr Gover" would likely be appointed -- the actual examiners were Orange and Clarke, not Orange and Gover. UPDATE THE OPEN QUESTIONS / MASTER NOTES: "Dr Gover" reference should be treated as the BMJ's speculation, not the confirmed examiner; "Dr Clarke" (a London physician, full name not yet established) was Orange's actual co-examiner.
KEY: The examination was NOT brief -- "Dr. Clarke and Dr. Orange... spent six or seven hours with the convict." This stands in sharp contrast to Savage's single examination of "a quarter or half an hour" before the trial, and is the central irony the BMJ's editorial (6 October, pp.682-683, already transcribed) implicitly draws on when calling for prolonged, proper examination rather than "a ten-minute examination... of a reticent or trembling wretch." The post-conviction examination that led to the reprieve was conducted with exactly the rigour the BMJ had been calling for since the trial -- vindicating their argument.
KEY NEW NAME: The prison chaplain at Chelmsford was the Rev. W. Faithfull Lumley. William "pays diligent attention to [his] ministrations." Worth investigating this figure further; chaplains' private observations of condemned prisoners were sometimes recorded and could be a further source.
KEY: Confirms William was visited "during the week" by "several of his relatives" -- consistent with Elizabeth's own account (Western Daily Press 21 Sept) of visiting with his sister and aunt, but indicates a broader pattern of family visits beyond just Elizabeth's documented visit.
