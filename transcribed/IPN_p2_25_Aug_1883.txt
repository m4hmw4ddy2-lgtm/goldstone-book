The constable said, "This is a charge of murder; this man has murdered his five children." The prisoner said, "That is right, sir. I did it like a man, too." I went to the house with Dr. Gould, and at about eight o'clock returned to the station and charged the prisoner with murdering his three children, Charles, Herbert, and Frederick, and with attempting to murder his two male infants. He said, "Quite right, sir. What condition is the missus in? I suppose some of you gentlemen have been around." Dr. Gould said, "Tolerable." The prisoner then asked, "Are the two young ones dead?" I said, "No." At half-past eight o'clock the following morning I charged him with the murder of the two younger children. He said, "Yes, sir. The other two are dead, then?" I said, "Yes." When I went to 8, Courtenay-place, I examined the cistern. It is about 6ft. from the floor, high up, near the ceiling. It was the top of it that was 6ft. from the floor. The top of it to the ceiling was about 10in. deep, the cistern being about 14in. deep.

By Mr. Atkinson: The prisoner volunteered these statements. He was a little excited, I thought, but very slight. He was not so excited as I should expect. He braced himself up when he said, "I did it like a man too." About two hours had elapsed since the prisoner last saw his wife. Then he asked me how she was.

Henry Wheatley, police-constable, 208 N, stationed at Lea-bridge, said: About a quarter to seven on the evening of the 8th inst. I was in the cell passage of the station leading to the cell where the prisoner was. The prisoner said to me, "I have had an extra drop of drink to-day to accomplish this job. There's five of them gone to glory, and it is a good job." Later on, at half-past seven o'clock, he said, "I am sorry for my wife and parents; but the children will be better off in Heaven, if there is such a place. It is better than leaving them to the mercy of the world." At about nine o'clock he said, "I wish I had killed the little ones out of the way. I don't know if I hit them once or twice. I have had this playing on my mind for a long time."

By Mr. Atkinson: He volunteered these statements. He was under my immediate observation all the time. I did not hear him rambling or mumbling to himself. I observed no traces of drink about the prisoner. He first stated he had killed the five, and then said he wished he had killed the little ones. I did not in the least observe any strangeness about him; he was very cool.

Mr. Henry Gould, examined by Mr. Batchelor, said: I reside at Lea-bridge-road, Leyton. I am a M.R.C.S., and L.S.A. London, and divisional surgeon of police for the district. On the 8th inst., at six o'clock, I was called by Inspector Foulkard, and went to 8, Courtenay-place. I went into the front room on the first floor, and there on a bed saw two children only a few days old. They were male children. I examined them, and found they had both received injuries to the head. I felt that the skull of each was fractured, and blood and froth were issuing from them. I thought it impossible that the children could live. I went into the kitchen, and there saw three children lying in a row, side by side, their feet being all one way. They were quite dead, and their clothes saturated with water. One of the children was naked, Dr. Twining having taken the clothes off. I saw no marks of violence on two of them, but on the eldest (Charles) I found on the neck an indentation mark of a stout cord. It had been put around the neck. At seven o'clock I again went to the house, and saw that one of the two infants had died. Soon after seven o'clock the next morning I went and found that the other one was dead. Since that time I have made post-mortem examinations of the bodies of the whole of the five children. As to the two younger, I found that their death had been caused by the violence used but that fractured the skull of each. As to the three others, from the condition of the heart and lungs, I came to the conclusion that they died from suffocation, caused by drowning. All the bodies of the three children were well nourished. On the three there were no marks of violence, except the indentation on the neck of the eldest.

By Mr. Atkinson: I have only seen the prisoner in connection with this case. I would not like to say that I am perfectly acquainted with the "Cyclopaedia of Practical Medicine."

Mr. Wells, the clerk, said this was scientific evidence of the doctor, and he was not well up in questions of insanity, as that was to be referred to, as that was a speciality.

Mr. Batchelor concurred with Mr. Wells that insanity was not a question for the consideration of this Court.

THE TRAGEDY AT WALTHAMSTOW.
[SUBJECT OF ILLUSTRATION.]

On Saturday, at the Stratford Petty Sessions, William Gouldstone, twenty-six years of age, described as a blacksmith, who lived at 8, Courtenay-place, St. James's-street, Walthamstow, was charged on remand with having murdered his five children on the 8th inst. -- namely, Charles Gouldstone, aged three years and a half; Herbert Gouldstone, two years and a half old; and Frederick Gouldstone, aged sixteen months, by drowning them in a cistern; and two twin infants, seven days old, by striking them on the head with a hammer. Mr. H. Batchelor conducted the prosecution on behalf of the Treasury authorities, and Mr. J. W. Atkinson, solicitor, Stratford, represented the accused.

The prisoner, against whom a coroner's jury has already returned a verdict of wilful murder, was placed in the dock shortly after eleven o'clock. After a hurried glance at the crowded court, he quietly folded his arms across the dock-rail, and while the charge was being read he again gazed around the court, nodding, with a smile, to some one whom he recognised. The accused, while maintaining an unconcerned aspect, so far as regarded the proceedings, very often changed his position. He would stretch across the dock, with his head resting on his folded arms on the bar in front of him; then he would stand erect, with great firmness; and next he would lean against the siderail of the dock, all the while, however, maintaining his cool demeanour. A tin mug full of water was brought into the dock for his use during the hearing, but he merely sipped a little water, and standing in the centre of the dock, rested his elbows on the rail till the case was concluded.

Mr. Batchelor, at the commencement of the proceedings, intimated that the evidence he would call would be very short, and he asked that the prisoner should be committed for trial.

Mrs. Hamilton, a lodger at 8, Courtenay-place, St. James's-street, and who attended as nurse upon Mrs. Gouldstone, was recalled and cross-examined by Mr. Atkinson. She said: I have known the prisoner since the 1st of June, and had ample opportunities to notice his demeanour. Up to this time he was always very kind to his wife. The twins were born on August 1, and after that date I noticed a great difference in the prisoner. I have never seen him the worse for drink but once. He was not addicted to drink. This page covers the Saturday 18 August committal hearing at Stratford in the clearest and most complete form found across all sources to date. The opening section (before the new "TRAGEDY AT WALTHAMSTOW" headline) is the continuation of testimony from Folkard, Wheatley, and Gould begun on the preceding page (not held in repository as a separate image, but the content is fully corroborated by, and adds detail to, the partial/garbled versions already transcribed from The Times 20 August and Bury Free Press 23 August).

KEY NEW NAME: "Dr. Twining" -- a previously unrecorded doctor who undressed Charles's body at the scene ("One of the children was naked, Dr. Twining having taken the clothes off"). This corrects/adds to the Morning Post inquest account (already transcribed), which attributed the undressing only to "the doctor and the constable" without naming Twining specifically. Cross-reference and reconcile: Twining may be the "doctor" referred to generically elsewhere, distinct from Henry Gould (the divisional police surgeon) and Dr Lyle (Elizabeth's physician).

KEY: Confirms precise cistern measurements again, with very slightly different phrasing from other sources but consistent figures: 6ft from the floor, 10in from the ceiling, 14in deep -- now corroborated across at least four independent sources (Old Bailey trial transcript, The Times 20 Aug, Bury Free Press 23 Aug, and this IPN account).

KEY: Folkard's cross-examination: "About two hours had elapsed since the prisoner last saw his wife. Then he asked me how she was." A small but human detail -- William's concern for Elizabeth surfacing again, consistent with his first question on arrest ("what condition is the missis in?").

KEY: William's demeanour in the dock at this hearing, described in unusually vivid detail: he "quietly folded his arms across the dock-rail," nodded with a smile to someone he recognised in the crowd, repeatedly changed position (resting his head on his folded arms, standing erect "with great firmness," leaning against the siderail), and "merely sipped a little water" from a tin mug provided for his use, maintaining what the reporter repeatedly calls his "cool demeanour" throughout. This is the most physically detailed eyewitness description of William's courtroom behaviour found in any source -- valuable for Part Three.

KEY: Confirms a coroner's jury had ALREADY returned a verdict of wilful murder by this hearing (18 August) -- consistent with the Morning Post inquest transcription (the resumed inquest, c.14 August) already transcribed.

KEY: Ada Hamilton's cross-examination here gives a SPECIFIC START DATE for her acquaintance with the family: "I have known the prisoner since the 1st of June" -- i.e. roughly ten weeks before the murders (8 August), consistent with her own "ten weeks" lodging claim elsewhere, and giving us a precise calendar date (1 June 1883) for when Ada and her husband began lodging with the Gouldstones.
