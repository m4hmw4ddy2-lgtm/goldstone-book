[THE KIRKCUDBRIGHTSHIRE ADVERTISER AND GALLOWAY NEWS]
28 September 1883 -- Editorial opinion piece

The sentence of death passed upon Gouldstone for the murder of his five children is causing the usual commotion and sympathy among the ranks of humanitarians. Let a more than usually atrocious criminal be condemned to the gallows, and these amiable individuals begin to move heaven and earth with the object of enabling the criminal to cheat the gallows. There are people about who believe the world to be populated by maniacs, and several of these sane individuals have decided that Gouldstone must have been mad or he would never have committed the murders. The same argument might be made to apply in the case of any murderer, whether he kills one or a dozen. No one, it may be said, would commit murder unless suffering at the time from some form of dementia. But there are many what may be called commonplace homicides who pass to their doom without a voice being heard to plead for their life. So why the claim for a remission of the death penalty should be pressed on behalf of one set of murderers, and especially withheld from others, has yet to be discovered -- unless we are to suppose that because a wretch deprives half-a-dozen innocent human beings of their life, he is worthy of six times the amount of sympathy that should be bestowed on him who slaughters one. That Gouldstone's poverty may have driven him to the extreme measures he took to save his children from future suffering might be pleaded; but there are hundreds and thousands of other fathers who are doubtless in exactly the same position as Gouldstone was, and who could never dream of doing as he did. There are, perhaps, few who could not advance testimony, if necessary, that they had some more or less remote relations known to be insane, or eccentric in some way. Most people will think that as long as the gallows is retained as a punishment for the crime of murder, there are few more worthy of the hangman's rope than the Walthamstow criminal. It is time that the public resolutely set its face against a system which causes sympathy to be aroused for atrocious murderers, because of the very magnitude of their crimes, while it leads to murderers of a commonplace character being consigned to their doom without a single effort being made on their behalf. In the case of Gouldstone the agitators have succeeded in persuading the Home Secretary to grant a reprieve for eight days, in order to ascertain whether the convict is really insane. If the medical report is in favour of sanity, he will expiate his crime on the 8th instead of the 1st October.

This is the clearest and most fully-articulated example found in the archive of the OPPOSING editorial position -- arguing AGAINST clemency for William, and against what the writer sees as a class-based or scale-based double standard in public sympathy for murderers. This is the necessary counterweight to the BMJ, Lancet, Times, and Law Times pieces, all of which argued for reprieve.

ESSENTIAL FOR PART THREE -- the book needs the full spectrum of contemporary opinion, and this is the strongest surviving statement of the "hang him anyway" position, articulated not as bloodthirsty rhetoric but as a reasoned argument about consistency and public policy.

KEY ARGUMENTS MADE:
- Scepticism that any "more or less remote" family history of insanity should excuse a crime, on the grounds that almost anyone could produce a similarly remote example: "There are, perhaps, few who could not advance testimony, if necessary, that they had some more or less remote relations known to be insane, or eccentric in some way."
- The "scale of sympathy" argument: a man who kills many is shown MORE sympathy than one who kills one, which the writer finds perverse: "he is worthy of six times the amount of sympathy that should be bestowed on him who slaughters one."
- Direct comparison to "hundreds and thousands of other fathers" in similarly difficult financial circumstances who never killed their children -- i.e. poverty alone cannot explain or excuse the act.
- Explicit framing of supporters of clemency as "agitators" who have "succeeded in persuading the Home Secretary."
- Confirms the original execution date was 1 October, respited by eight days to 8 October pending the medical inquiry (NOTE: other sources, e.g. Sunday Dispatch, give slightly different precise dates for the original/respited execution -- treat 1 October as the original date and 8 October as the respited date per this source, cross-referenced against the Guardian 26 Sept piece which also gives "the 8th October").

This piece should be read in Part Three immediately alongside or in contrast to the BMJ's "scandal to law" framing -- they represent the two poles of contemporary public debate, both responding to the identical set of facts.
