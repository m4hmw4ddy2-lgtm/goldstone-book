THE WALTHAMSTOW MURDER.
Western Daily Press, 21 September 1883

Mrs Gouldstone, who is exerting herself to the utmost to save her unfortunate husband's life, writes to state her solemn belief that when the crime was committed the convict was not responsible for his act. She says:

"During our five years of married life I frequently noticed that my husband was absent-minded and given to brooding. If he were asked a question he would not, no matter how simple it was, or however easy to answer, reply at once. He would think over it, and sometimes say nothing concerning the question. We commenced housekeeping on 21s. a week, and lately we had 25s. It was sometimes difficult to make ends meet when the children came so rapidly, and this weighed on his mind, I fear, although he never spoke to it to me -- except in the way of saying how sorry he was that my hands were so full, and could not go out together as we used to do. That was the only way in which he ever spoke of the burden of the family. Before we had the first child, and right on till the birth of the second, we regularly went to church. The second child was weakly, and the first was still only a baby, so that we could not leave the house together. For all his occasional absent ways he was very lively, and as I had all to do with the spending of the money, and did not let him know if there was any trouble about payments when there was a confinement or sickness in the house, I always found it very easy to turn his attention to something brighter. He was very kindly with me and the children, handy and willing if they were ailing, and he constantly helped me in household duties. He was a man who had few acquaintances except his own relatives and his fellow-workmen, and he was nearly always at home when not at work, except on the Sunday morning, when he occasionally took a walk alone. But for hours together he would keep the children going with his concertina, which he was very fond of playing, and I was engaged in household duties. The children were very fond of him and were never tired of listening to the concertina. If he seemed worried, amusing the children -- at which he was very good -- or a remark from me would at once change his mood. He was painfully aware of how easy it was for him to get worried, and he has often said to me that it was a good job he had not much to think of, as if he had believed it would turn his brain. Nothing that ever he said or did before the loss of the little ones gave me the least fear that he would take his own life or that of any one else. But shortly before his last babes were born he said to me that he believed he had not many weeks to live, which was a strange remark that I have often thought of since. He never complained, except that he did not know how he believed he would manage with my hands already so full, and soon to be even fuller. But then lots of people manage on less, and we did very well, and when I put before him this trouble before him the question ceased to be moody and absent. I never knew till very lately that there was any insanity in the family.

Since the occurrence I have seen my husband three times -- once at the House of Detention, once at the Old Bailey, and yesterday (Tuesday) in the prison at Chelmsford. On each occasion he has been as happy as a child at play. At Chelmsford he said he had seen several relatives whom he had not met for a long time, but neither he nor I made any reference to the fate of the children. His sister and aunt accompanied me yesterday. He asked me to take care of myself, and, turning to his relatives, said, 'You will take care of her, won't you?' Then he said, 'I have broken God's commandment, and I must expect to suffer, but He will forgive me for what I have done.' He realises his position in so far as he believes he will die, but he seems to have no notion of the enormity of killing the children he spoke of them as being in heaven, where he hopes soon to join them. Here is a letter I have from him, dated the 15th, in the same strain: --

My Dear Wife -- I feel it is a Pleasure to Write a few Lines to you and trusting to God that it will find you as well as Can be Expected after the Dreadfull news but I am thankfull to God that I feel happy now as I may Expect and I Hope you will Look to God to Help and Comfort you as Well as myself Dearest Wife I Hope you wont fret much about me as What I Hope you Will Receive this Letter this Evening and than I Hope you will Be able to Come and See Me Over a Short Time and I Hope and All at Home are quite Well and All Cousins and Remember me to all that know me, and Let Cousin Gouldstone in Mash Street know How I am and I am going to send a Few Lines to Mr G. Sampford and I Hope all Will forgive me for I am thinking about you All But I Hope you Will Pray to God to Comfort me and Bear that in mind that God Will forgive all I have Done But I Hope you Will Live Upon my Dear Wife for I Can Have What I Wish for in my Living from the Prison and you may Believe that the Chappelling will Give me All I Require and I thank God that I have been preparing for the Other World and of which I shell Do more so now and Live in Hopes to meet you All in Heaven. So Good By and God Bless You
WILLIAM GOULDSTONE.

Her Majesty's Prison, Chelmsford, Essex, September 15th, 1883.

I have no doubt he did not know and could not help what he did. He was always most affectionate to me and the children, and I cannot think that he should suffer a shameful death for what would be to him, if he only understood it, the most awful distress."

TRANSCRIPTION NOTES
Source: Western Daily Press, 21 September 1883
Repository image: sources/images/Media-coverage/Western_Daily_Press_21_Sep.jpg
This is the most significant document found in the entire Media-coverage archive: Elizabeth's own full press statement, and William's complete letter from Chelmsford Prison, dated 15 September 1883, the day after the guilty verdict. This is almost certainly THE document the BMJ refers to on 29 September 1883 as "the touching narrative of his wife."

ESSENTIAL FOR PART THREE AND FOR THE COLLAPSED-AND-RECOVERING ELIZABETH OF PART FOUR.

KEY DETAILS FROM ELIZABETH'S STATEMENT:
- They married and were five years into married life at the time -- consistent with the 15 March 1879 marriage date and 8 August 1883 murders (just under 4.5 years; Elizabeth says "five years," likely rounding, or counting from courtship).
- They began housekeeping on 21 shillings a week, "lately we had 25s." Consistent with Graves's Old Bailey testimony that William earned 25s a week.
- William was "absent-minded and given to brooding" THROUGHOUT their marriage, not just after the twins were born -- a significant reframing. This was a longstanding trait, not a new development.
- They attended church regularly "before we had the first child, and right on till the birth of the second" -- religious practice lapsed after the second child (i.e., after Herbert's birth, c.1881-82).
- William played the concertina to entertain the children for hours -- NEW DETAIL, not found in any other source. Combine with the IPN's claim that he played cornet and flute: William was evidently a genuinely accomplished amateur musician across multiple instruments, and used music actively with his children.
- "He has often said to me that it was a good job he had not much to think of, as if he had believed it would turn his brain" -- William himself articulated, before the murders, his fear that worry/thinking too much could "turn his brain."
- CRITICAL NEW DETAIL: "shortly before his last babes were born he said to me that he believed he had not many weeks to live, which was a strange remark that I have often thought of since." William predicted his own death in the weeks before the twins were born. This is entirely new and of major significance for Part One/Two -- it supports the BMJ's later claim that he intended to drown himself, and suggests this was not a sudden idea on 8 August but something building for weeks.
- "I never knew till very lately that there was any insanity in the family" -- Elizabeth herself only learned of the hereditary insanity after the murders, during the legal proceedings.
- Elizabeth visited William three times: at the House of Detention, at the Old Bailey, and at Chelmsford Prison on Tuesday 18 September 1883 (the Tuesday following the Friday 14 September verdict).
- William's sister and aunt accompanied Elizabeth on the Chelmsford visit -- the same aunt referenced in the BMJ's "his mother and aunt were both insane" -- she was well enough, or considered appropriate, to make this visit. Worth reconciling with the insanity claim; she may be a different aunt, or a relative by marriage rather than blood.
- William's words to his relatives at Chelmsford: "You will take care of her, won't you?" Then: "I have broken God's commandment, and I must expect to suffer, but He will forgive me for what I have done."
- "he seems to have no notion of the enormity of killing the children he spoke of them as being in heaven, where he hopes soon to join them" -- confirms his persistent belief, post-verdict, that the children are in heaven and that this was a positive outcome.

WILLIAM'S LETTER FROM CHELMSFORD PRISON, 15 SEPTEMBER 1883 -- FULL TEXT NOW RECOVERED:
Transcribed above with original capitalisation and spelling preserved as closely as possible to the source (William's own irregular capitalisation of nouns mid-sentence is a feature of the original and should be preserved in any direct quotation, as it is evidence of his level of education/literacy).
KEY PHRASES: "I feel it is a Pleasure to Write a few Lines to you" -- opens warmly, almost formally.
"I am thankfull to God that I feel happy now" -- consistent with all his other recorded statements of happiness since the murders.
"Let Cousin Gouldstone in Mash Street know How I am" -- a previously unknown relative, "Cousin Gouldstone," living on/at "Mash Street" (likely Marsh Street, Walthamstow, renamed High Street in December 1882 -- William may be using the old, locally familiar name even after the official renaming, or this could be a different Marsh/Mash Street). WORTH INVESTIGATING -- a cousin of William's living locally in Walthamstow, not previously known.
"I am going to send a Few Lines to Mr G. Sampford" -- "Sampford" is almost certainly a reference to Great Sampford, William's home parish in Essex, possibly addressed to a specific person there (a "Mr G." of Sampford) or possibly the start of a sentence about writing home to Sampford that has been mis-transcribed/compressed by the newspaper. WORTH FURTHER INVESTIGATION.
"I shell Do more so now and Live in Hopes to meet you All in Heaven" -- closing sentiment, consistent with his stated belief that the murdered children await him in heaven.
The letter is signed and dated: "WILLIAM GOULDSTONE. Her Majesty's Prison, Chelmsford, Essex, September 15th, 1883."

ELIZABETH'S CLOSING STATEMENT (also separately quoted in Dundee Courier and Argus 21 Sept, already noted in earlier session notes): "I have no doubt he did not know and could not help what he did. He was always most affectionate to me and the children, and I cannot think that he should suffer a shameful death for what would be to him, if he only understood it, the most awful distress."

THIS DOCUMENT RESOLVES OPEN QUESTION 2.2 (Elizabeth's narrative) FROM THE OPEN QUESTIONS FILE. It does not fully resolve whether a longer or more formal statement was separately submitted to the Home Secretary (the BMJ's "touching narrative" reference could still mean a separate Home Office submission), but this published press statement is now the primary available text and should be used as Elizabeth's own words throughout Part Three and Part Four. The Home Office HO 144 file should still be sought to confirm whether this is identical to, or distinct from, what was submitted to Harcourt.
