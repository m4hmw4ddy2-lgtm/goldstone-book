[Continuing Ada Hamilton's cross-examination by Mr Atkinson:]

...the 1st of June, and had ample opportunities to notice his demeanour. Up to this time he was always very kind to his wife. The twins were born on August 1, and after that date I noticed a great difference in the prisoner. I have never seen him the worse for drink but once. He was not addicted to drink. After August 1 he appeared to be in a desponding state. He would come in and sit down and seldom speak to any one. I have never heard him speak a thing against his wife, and never heard him complain of her pawning his things. He was very kind to her, and I have never known him to speak a cross word to her. I have never heard his wife say that she wished the children were dead. Since the birth of these twins I have noticed he appeared absent. I never said anything to witness about a fit of kleptomania coming on him. He did not speak to that effect on the Sunday previous. As to the twins I heard him say nothing when I was by his wife's side. When he was sitting on the fireguard in the kitchen just before the murder he was not gazing into the fire. He had his back to it, and was twiddling a piece of string about. I did not notice that there was a slip noose in the cord. It may have been there, but I did not notice it. He looked in the bedroom and muttered something. I saw him in the bedroom after the occurrence, and I did not notice a hammer till afterwards, when the constable asked for it. After the death of the three children I said, "Oh, you wicked man, what have you done?" He replied, "I am happy now." I saw one of the children first, and shortly afterwards the other two. I went to see if they were dead or alive, when I saw him carrying the first one. He seemed very excited -- not absent or wandering, but excited. He said, "I've done it, I've done it." When the constable took him into the bedroom, he said to Mrs. Gouldstone, "I have murdered all your children; you wished them dead, and now you have got your wish." I do not know any of his family.

The context is unclear without seeing the original cross-examination question Ada is answering, but the existence of this exchange means the word and concept were genuinely in circulation regarding William's case at the time, NOT a later AI fabrication. Treat with care: it does not mean William had kleptomania, only that the question/rumour was put to Ada under cross-examination and she denied any such suggestion.

2. "He had his back to it, and was twiddling a piece of string about. I did not notice that there was a slip noose in the cord. It may have been there, but I did not notice it." NEW: the cord/string William was handling in the kitchen may have been tied in a SLIP NOOSE. Ada herself raises this under cross-examination, conceding she did not notice at the time but allowing it may have been there. This is a significant detail -- if the cord was tied as a noose, it strengthens the reading (already suggested by the "pointed to his throat" detail in the Daily Chronicle 13 August) that William's preoccupation with the cord/string was connected to thoughts of his own death by hanging or strangulation, predating and perhaps prefiguring his eventual execution by the state, or connected to a private suicidal preoccupation.

3. "I have never heard his wife say that she wished the children were dead." This is Ada's own testimony on the same point Clarke addressed in her coroner deposition (see The Times 11 Aug transcription notes) -- Ada FLATLY DENIES ever hearing Elizabeth say this, in apparent tension with Clarke's account (which qualified the claim but did not deny it outright). This is a genuine discrepancy between the two principal female witnesses and should be flagged clearly: Clarke says she heard it (but did not think Elizabeth was in earnest, and only when the children were being difficult); Ada says she never heard it at all. The BMJ's amplification of this point (22 September) into a causal factor in William's decision now looks even less secure given this direct contradiction. STRONGLY RECOMMEND the book either omits this claim or handles it with explicit, careful sourcing given the conflicting witness testimony.

4. Ada's fuller account of William's words on being confronted: "I am happy now" (shorter than other versions) followed later by, when seeing the first child found: "I've done it, I've done it" -- repeated twice. And to Elizabeth specifically, in the bedroom with the constable present: "I have murdered all your children; you wished them dead, and now you have got your wish." This is the clearest, most complete rendering of this specific accusatory statement to Elizabeth -- combining the "you wished them dead" line directly with "murdered all your children" in a single sentence, attributed by Ada under formal cross-examination (a more reliable context than casual press paraphrase).

5. "I do not know any of his family." Ada's own admission that despite ten weeks of close lodging with the family, she had no acquaintance with William's relatives -- consistent with the picture of the Gouldstones as a self-contained, somewhat isolated couple.

6. Final committal: "the prisoner was committed for trial, Mr. Atkinson intimating that the accused would reserve his defence." Confirms formal committal to the Old Bailey at the conclusion of this 18 August hearing.
