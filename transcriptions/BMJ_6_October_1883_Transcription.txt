THE BRITISH MEDICAL JOURNAL
Medical News, 6 October 1883.

It is understood that Dr Orange, the Medical Superintendent of Broadmoor, who was recently sent down to Chelmsford to see the convict Gouldstone, entertained no doubt of his insanity. His mother and aunt were both insane and his great-uncle committed suicide. He himself appears to have had a suicidal mania, and it is believed that he went out with the intention of drowning himself on the very day that he murdered his children. His wife, who is a very respectable young woman, says that he has shown signs of madness for the last twelve months.

The British Medical Journal, Medical News, 6 October 1883.

KEY DETAILS FOR THE BOOK — MAJOR NEW MATERIAL

1. HE WENT OUT TO DROWN HIMSELF.
"It is believed that he went out with the intention of drowning himself on the very day that he murdered his children."
This is entirely new — not in any witness statement, trial testimony, or previous press coverage found to date. William left the house on 8 August 1883 intending to drown himself. He failed. He came home with a piece of cord in his pocket.

IMPLICATIONS:
- The cistern was already in his mind as an instrument of death before he came home — or he had intended to use a river, pond, or other water source.
- The chair placed under the cistern may have been intended for himself before it became the means of reaching the children.
- He came home having failed to die. He came home to his five children.
- This reframes the prologue, Part One, and Part Two. The cord in his pocket was not brought for the children — it was brought for himself.
- Explains the cell statement "I wish I had killed the little ones out of the way" — he wished the sequence had been different; he had tried to die first and failed.
- The fact that he used the cistern — the same water source he had intended for himself — may not be coincidental.

SOURCE RELIABILITY: "It is believed" — not directly attributed. May derive from Dr Orange's examination of William at Chelmsford, from Elizabeth's account to the Home Office, or both. Treat as well-sourced inference pending primary source confirmation.

ACTION REQUIRED: National Archives, Kew, HO 144 series (Home Office registered papers, capital cases) — look for Orange's examination report and Home Office papers for the Gouldstone case. This is potentially the most important undiscovered primary source in the project.

2. FAMILY HISTORY OF INSANITY — EXPANDED.
"His mother and aunt were both insane and his great-uncle committed suicide."
- Mother's insanity confirmed (previously known from trial testimony).
- Aunt insane — NEW. Not previously recorded.
- Great-uncle's suicide — NEW. Not previously recorded.
- Three immediate or close relatives with serious mental illness or suicide. The hereditary argument the BMJ made on 22 September is now given specific names and relationships.

3. TWELVE MONTHS OF SIGNS.
"His wife... says that he has shown signs of madness for the last twelve months."
- Onset approximately October 1882 — a full year before the murders.
- Predates the pregnancy with the twins (Elizabeth would have conceived around November 1882).
- Predates any financial pressure from an additional child.
- The twins were not the cause of William's deterioration. They were the trigger for something already long established.
- Elizabeth is the source — this is part of the narrative she gave to the Home Office or press, referred to in the 29 September BMJ as "the touching narrative of his wife."

4. DR ORANGE AT CHELMSFORD.
Dr Orange, Medical Superintendent of Broadmoor, was sent to Chelmsford Prison to examine Gouldstone after the death sentence. He entertained no doubt of his insanity. This examination directly preceded and almost certainly caused the reprieve of 3 October 1883. Orange will be responsible for William for the next 52 years.

5. ELIZABETH — "A VERY RESPECTABLE YOUNG WOMAN."
The BMJ's characterisation of Elizabeth. She has been in contact with the Home Office or the press or both since the trial. Her account is shaping the medical and public response. She is not a passive figure in the aftermath.
