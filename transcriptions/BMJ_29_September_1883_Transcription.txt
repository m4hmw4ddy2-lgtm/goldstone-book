THE BRITISH MEDICAL JOURNAL
29 September 1883.

THE WALTHAMSTOW MURDER — SECOND NOTICE.

We are informed that the Home Secretary intends to commute the sentence on the convict Gouldstone to penal servitude for life, the evidence of insanity being sufficient to justify a modification of his punishment. It is satisfactory to find that the Home Secretary has promptly yielded to the representations made to him and ordered an inquiry into the mental condition of the convict. The names of the physicians to whom the inquiry has been trusted have not been announced, but the probability is that Dr Orange of Broadmoor and Dr Gover, Her Majesty's Inspector of Prisons, who are generally employed by the Home Office in such investigations, have been instructed to examine Gouldstone. It would be well that the terms of the reference to them should be made public, for no investigation limited to the convict's present mental state, and that did not include his mental state at the time when he committed the crime for which he has been condemned to death, could be regarded as in the least degree satisfactory. Evidence in support of the view that Gouldstone was insane when he destroyed his children has been accumulating rapidly since we commented on the case; and no medical man can read the touching narrative of his wife and the clear statement of the Vicar of the parish in which he lived without finding them in strong corroboration of the only theory that is consistent with the facts of the case as given in evidence — the theory that the man whose arm, when lifted against the children who were so dear to him, was moved not by criminal design or passion but by the dire compulsion of disease. The "irresponsible frivolity" and obstinate ignorance of journalism have, in one or two instances, led to sanguinary demands for the "penalty and forfeit" of the law; but, as a rule, the lay and even legal press have recognised the true significance of symptoms in this case, while the organs of medical opinion have been unanimous in their demand for a diagnosis made by competent authorities.

The British Medical Journal, 29 September 1883.

KEY DETAILS FOR THE BOOK (Part Three, Chapter Seven):

1. DR ORANGE OF BROADMOOR. The Home Office is instructing Dr Orange — superintendent of Broadmoor — to examine Gouldstone. This is the man who will be responsible for William for the next fifty-two years. His name is now in the record and belongs in Part Three.

2. THE VICAR'S STATEMENT. The BMJ refers to "the clear statement of the Vicar of the parish in which he lived." A vicar made a statement about William's mental state sufficiently substantial to be cited alongside Elizabeth's own narrative. THIS SOURCE HAS NOT BEEN FOUND. Which parish — St James's Street Walthamstow, or Great Sampford where William grew up? This is a potentially major undiscovered source. Add to open questions.

3. ELIZABETH'S NARRATIVE. The BMJ refers to "the touching narrative of his wife." This implies a statement by Elizabeth substantially longer than her brief press statement, which reached the Home Secretary and the medical press. THIS SOURCE HAS NOT BEEN FOUND. May be in magistrates' court proceedings, Home Office papers, or a newspaper account we have not yet located. Potentially one of the most important undiscovered sources in the project. Add to open questions.

4. THE REPRIEVE. The Home Secretary (Sir William Harcourt) is intending to commute the sentence to penal servitude for life. The BMJ notes this with satisfaction but urges that the inquiry examine his mental state at the time of the crime, not only his present mental state. This is published 15 days after the death sentence and 18 days before the formal reprieve (3 October 1883).

5. THE PRESS RESPONSE. The BMJ distinguishes between irresponsible journalism calling for execution ("penalty and forfeit" of the law) and the lay and legal press, which the BMJ says has largely recognised the true significance of the symptoms. The medical press unanimous in calling for proper diagnosis.

6. "THE DIRE COMPULSION OF DISEASE." The BMJ's formulation for what moved William's arm. This phrase belongs in Part Three.
