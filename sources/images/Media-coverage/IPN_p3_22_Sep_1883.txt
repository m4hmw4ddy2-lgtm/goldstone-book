THE WALTHAMSTOW TRAGEDY -- SENTENCE OF DEATH.
Illustrated Police News, 22 September 1883

At the Central Criminal Court on Friday, before Mr. Justice Day, William Gouldstone, twenty-five, whitesmith, was indicted for the wilful murder of his five children -- Charles Gouldstone, Herbert Gouldstone, Frederick Gouldstone, and two infants -- at Walthamstow on the 8th ultimo. The prisoner, on being placed in the dock, glanced carelessly around the court, and pleaded "Not guilty" in a calm voice.

After a lengthy cross-examination of the witnesses, Mr. Grain proceeded to sum up the case for the defence. The history of it was short and simple, he said, but heartrending and awful. A young man, well conducted, was married five years ago, and during that time had gained the esteem of his employers, the confidence of his fellow-workmen, and the love of his family. Under these circumstances, it seemed a matter of impossibility that a sane man could have committed the terrible crime. There was no evidence of want, but, on the contrary, every sign of those small comforts that people in their sphere of life might look for. There had been no threat of murdering the children, though of taking his own life. The learned counsel went on through the medical evidence, and asked the jury if they must not come to the conclusion that this was a sudden visitation of Providence, unhinging a brain, either from hereditary madness, bad health, or acute pain. After knowing what sort of man the prisoner had been, he asked them to say that on the evidence for the prosecution they would feel they were satisfying the ends of justice, the protection of the public, and the protection of human life by finding when he committed these acts for which he was being tried for his life he was of unsound mind. When they retired he besought them each individually to turn over the whole of the evidence, to consider carefully everything that had been urged, and give their verdict conscientiously.

Mr. Poland, replying on behalf of the Crown, said the issue they had to try was whether the prisoner at the bar, when he committed the crime, knew what he was doing, and that it was against the law of the land. Here was a man, twenty-six years of age, one of seven children, working for years at one place, and never until August 8 showing any signs of insanity. It was ridiculous to imagine that because a man when suffering from pain said he wished he was dead he was insane. Up to the evening of the crime, when the prisoner returned to the house, there had not been the slightest symptoms of insanity, and he was then cool and collected. Was this a sudden visitation, or did the evidence rather show that this terrible crime was the result of calm deliberation. He said to the policeman, "I am ready for the rope," and afterwards told him that a week previously he thought of buying a revolver for the purpose, but abandoned it. It was not rather this cool deliberation of a man who had determined to end a struggle for existence and take the consequences, for which he had nerved himself, as he afterwards stated, by taking drink. The question they had to determine was whether the prisoner really knew what he was doing; and if they did that, he could hardly conceive the verdict being anything but adverse to the prisoner. The jury must face the whole facts of the evidence, and if they were satisfied that the prisoner was responsible for the deed, then, but then alone, would he ask them to give their verdict for the Crown.

Mr. Justice Day, in summing up the case to the jury, observed that the point they had to decide -- as there could be no doubt that the prisoner had destroyed the lives of his five children in the way stated -- was not whether the prisoner was sane or insane, but whether at the time he committed the crime he was in such a condition of mind as not to know that he was killing his children or that he was not doing wrong. This had been solemnly decided many years ago by the law, and he therefore informed them as to the real question they had to decide. The learned judge then directed the attention of the jury to the medical evidence, and to the fact that it was admitted by Dr. Savage that he had no doubt that when the prisoner killed his children he knew perfectly well what he was committing the crime of murder, and that death was the penalty for that crime. His lordship, in conclusion, explained that under the provisions of a recent Act of Parliament, if they found that the prisoner was insane at the time he committed the act, they would have to return a special verdict stating that the prisoner did commit the offence, but that he was insane at the time.

The jury retired at twenty minutes past five o'clock to deliberate upon their verdict. After being absent about a quarter of an hour they returned and found the prisoner guilty of wilful murder.

Mr. Justice Day, having put on the black cap, addressed the prisoner, and said that he had been convicted upon overwhelming evidence of the crime of wilful murder, and the jury had no alternative but to find him guilty of that offence. It was not his practice to make any observations that might add to the misery of a man in his position, and all he should do was to exhort him to seek for mercy from the quarter from which alone it could be obtained. His lordship, who was a good deal affected, then passed sentence of death in the usual form, and the prisoner, who presented a perfectly calm and composed appearance, was removed from the bar.

TRANSCRIPTION NOTES
Source: The Illustrated Police News, 22 September 1883, p.3
Repository image: sources/images/Media-coverage/IPN p3 Sep 22 1883.png
This is a clear, full account of the Old Bailey trial and verdict (14 September 1883), covering the closing speeches of defence counsel Mr Grain and prosecution counsel Mr Poland, Mr Justice Day's summing-up, the jury's deliberation and verdict, and sentencing.
NOTE: This source gives William's age as TWENTY-FIVE in the opening line, but TWENTY-SIX in Poland's speech later in the same article. The indictment and all other authoritative sources confirm 26. Treat the "twenty-five" in the opening line as a compositor error.
KEY: Mr Grain's defence speech: "A young man, well conducted, was married five years ago, and during that time had gained the esteem of his employers, the confidence of his fellow-workmen, and the love of his family... There had been no threat of murdering the children, though of taking his own life." This is a clear defence-counsel assertion, IN COURT, that William had previously threatened to take his OWN life, not the children's -- corroborating testimony from Cakebread, Skelton, and Bennett Gouldstone about the lift-hole and train threats.
KEY: Mr Poland for the prosecution: explicitly cites William's statement "I am ready for the rope" and the revolver admission as evidence of "cool deliberation of a man who had determined to end a struggle for existence and take the consequences, for which he had nerved himself, as he afterwards stated, by taking drink." This is the prosecution's own characterisation that William intended to end "a struggle for existence" -- i.e. that William's OWN death (not just the children's) was bound up in his plan, even in the prosecution's reading, though the prosecution drew the opposite conclusion (premeditation/sanity) from this same evidence that the defence and later medical commentary drew (suicidal intent/insanity).
KEY: Confirms the precise legal standard given to the jury by Justice Day, and the operation of the Trial of Lunatics Act 1883 ("a recent Act of Parliament") under which a "guilty but insane" special verdict was possible -- though the jury did not return this verdict; they returned a straightforward guilty verdict, with insanity only later being accepted by the Home Secretary as grounds for reprieve.
KEY: William's demeanour at sentencing: "the prisoner, who presented a perfectly calm and composed appearance, was removed from the bar." Justice Day himself "was a good deal affected" when passing sentence.
