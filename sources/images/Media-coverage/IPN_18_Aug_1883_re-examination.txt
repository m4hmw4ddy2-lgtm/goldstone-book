When the murderer was taken away by the poor wife, perhaps fortunately, lost consciousness for an hour; and through the night, although broken into ticks, she would survive, an alternately broke into ticks and swoon again. Two doctors have been in attendance since it occurred, allowing nature to take its own course. They have been supplying her with jellies and light strengthening food. She asked and begged to see her dead little ones, but of course was denied. Some incautious people, with nothing but their own idle curiosity to satisfy, invaded the house and asked to see the poor woman, but of course were denied. The police, anticipating a crowd of sightseers, have volunteered a good body of their staff to keep the press back, but it is to be hoped their services will not be required, and respect will be had for the woman's dangerous condition.

The scene of the murder is a smart two-storeyed house, with a bay-windowed parlour. It is parallel with St. James's-street Railway-station. The part of Walthamstow in which it is situated is a thin, straggling neighbourhood, and the house, though one of the first of its row, has only been built two years.

RE-EXAMINATION ON SATURDAY LAST.

The prisoner, William Gouldstone, was again brought up at Stratford on Saturday morning. While in the cells of the court he was visited by two of his brothers. He seemed calm and perfectly unconcerned at his position.

When placed in the dock the prisoner immediately folded his arms and rested them on the front. He listened intently to the charge as read by Mr. Powell, the chairman. Mr. Atkinson appeared for the prisoner.

Mr. Wells, the clerk, read over the depositions of the witness Ada Hamilton, the nurse, furtively glanced alternately at the Bench, the reporters, and the people in the court, as crowded.
