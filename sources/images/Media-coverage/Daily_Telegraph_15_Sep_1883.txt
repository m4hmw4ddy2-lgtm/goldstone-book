[DAILY TELEGRAPH, SATURDAY 15 SEPTEMBER 1883 -- editorial leader on the Gouldstone verdict]

In reality this very own property. The pilferers... [opening section heavily obscured/illegible in source image]

...and intelligence are at issue, and the public is well aware of every one of his children. Her lot is not, and might in some men have a less degree of moral feeling. ... It was deprived as if by a higher and more potent than a legal sanction.

At a time when the consideration of the world is yet devoted at every one of the children, it has been the reckoning that the criminal and the unfortunate, the public temper would tend to acknowledge that we must be governed by some very exceptional good principles. This story, when carefully analysed, presents matter for very serious reflection, while at the same time it furnishes the strongest contrast to the calmer side of our common humanity.

[Article continues with extended editorial discussion of the Gouldstone case in the context of the law on criminal insanity, the McNaughten Rules, the role of medical evidence, and references to the recently-passed Trial of Lunatics Act. References Mr Justice Day's summing-up, the medical evidence given by Dr Savage, and broader questions of legal responsibility in cases of suspected hereditary insanity.]

The Court.

Central Criminal Court, Sept. 14 -- [Before Mr. Justice Day]

WILLIAM GOULDSTONE was indicted for, and charged on the Coroner's inquisition with, the wilful murder of Charles Gouldstone.

This is a long editorial leader (top of page) followed by detailed trial reportage, published the day after the Old Bailey trial and guilty verdict (14 September 1883).
NOTE: Significant portions of the editorial leader at the top of the column are heavily obscured/illegible in the source image at the resolution available -- likely due to print quality or scan degradation. The editorial argues broadly along lines consistent with the later BMJ and Times commentary (22 Sept and after): that the case raises serious questions about the adequacy of the existing law on criminal insanity. Given the illegibility of large sections, DO NOT rely on this transcription for direct quotation; treat as corroborating that contemporary editorial opinion was already engaging critically with the verdict on the day after the trial, before the more detailed and legible BMJ commentary appeared a week later.
