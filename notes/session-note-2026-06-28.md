## PROLOGUE — FINAL TEXT

On the evening of Wednesday the 8th of August 1883, William Gouldstone came home from work two hours early. He had not been at work.

Elizabeth Gouldstone, already a mother to three boys, had given birth to twins seven days earlier. She was lying in the back bedroom of 8 Courtenay Place, St James's Street, Walthamstow, while Ada Hamilton washed the babies at her bedside. Ada and her husband had been lodging in the rooms above for ten weeks already and she had been engaged as nurse for Elizabeth's confinement. The three older boys, Charles, Herbert, and Frederick, were in the kitchen.

Unexpectedly, Ada heard someone enter the house. She put down the baby and went to see who was there.

William was standing by the dresser. He was twenty-six years old, and his usual return from the ironworks wasn't until half past seven. It was a quarter past five.

Ada said: "You did frighten me. I wondered who it was."

He smiled but made no reply. He had been like this since the twins were born, strange and withdrawn, and she had learned not to press him. She went back to the bedroom, finished washing the first twin and laid him next to his mother and brother.

When she returned to the kitchen, William had settled himself on the fire guard. The three boys were playing on the floor around him and he was watching them, saying nothing. He had a length of cord in his fingers, turning it slowly. She asked whether he had had his tea yet, and he replied only, "No." She told him it was ready but he made no reply to that at all, so she went back to see to Elizabeth and the babies.

Once she had finished washing the second twin, as she had done all afternoon, she went back through the kitchen toward the scullery. The boys were still playing. William was still on the fire guard, still watching them, the cord still in his hands. In the scullery, she noticed that the perambulator had been moved from its place beside the cistern and a chair put there instead. The cistern sat six feet from the floor. It held fourteen inches of water.

She turned to ask who had moved the chair. William heard her, but it was three-year-old Charles who answered: "My daddy put it there."

Back in the bedroom, Ada helped Elizabeth out of bed so it could be remade, then helped her back in. When William appeared in the doorway she told him he could come in, everyone was decent, and he mumbled something she could not make out before going back to the kitchen. She suggested to Elizabeth that she might leave them alone for a few minutes, to give William the chance to speak to his wife privately. Elizabeth agreed.

Downstairs, just as Ada reached the back door, she heard screams of "Murder!" from above.

She ran back up and tried the bedroom door, but it was locked from the inside. She tried to force it and could not. Elizabeth was screaming through it: "Open the door. He is murdering my children."

Ada cried out for help.

*I wasn't looking for William Gouldstone. I was looking for someone else entirely. But this is the story I found. A murder that shocked the nation and that created my family.*

## KEY DECISIONS ON THE PROLOGUE

- Opens with two sentences establishing premeditation before the scene begins. *He had not been at work* is the first revelation of the book — he told Elizabeth he left when the manager went to dinner, but he had not been at work at all.
- Ends on the locked door. Everything behind it belongs to Part Two.
- Cistern measurements (six feet from floor, fourteen inches of water, from Folkard's statement) included at the moment Ada notices the chair. Reader can do the arithmetic.
- Free indirect style used for Ada's discomfort — *strange and withdrawn, and she had learned not to press him.*
- The *still* construction used three times in the scullery paragraph — cord still in his hands, still on the fire guard, still watching — to establish William's wrongness through stillness against the movement of the boys.
- Four sentences in Rik's own voice close the prologue, surfacing the researcher briefly before disappearing — the structural equivalent of Summerscale's use of Whicher in *The Suspicions of Mr Whicher.*
- Em-dashes forbidden throughout. None present.
- Boys referred to as him/his throughout — the twins were male.

## THE HOUSE — CURRENT STATE OF KNOWLEDGE

### Address
8 Courtenay Place, St James's Street, Walthamstow — the contemporaneous address used in all 1883 documents. This is the address to use in the book.

2 Helen Cottages, Queens Road, West Walthamstow — from William's 1935 coroner's report, compiled 52 years after the fact. Queens Road did not exist in 1883 — it was consolidated from Queen's Road East and Queen's Road West in 1903. The 1935 report used a post-1903 street name. This may refer to the same building under a different naming system, or may be a different address entirely.

The current Courtenay Place, E17 dates from 1893 — ten years after the murders — and is a different street from the 1883 address.

### What stands there now
NLS historic OS map (1890s) shows Courtenay Place as a short terrace of houses immediately north of St James Street station, between the station and St James's Street, running diagonally off the main road. The railway line runs directly behind where the houses stood.

Satellite overlay confirms the site is now a bus depot. The terrace is gone. The walking tour blog claim (previously treated as hearsay) is confirmed by the map evidence.

### Warner Estate connection
NOT CONFIRMED. The Warner Estate Company was not registered until 1891. The murder happened in 1883. The Courtenay Place name is associated with Warner's later Courtenay Building Ltd, but that does not confirm he developed the original 1883 street. The Liverpool Mercury describes the house as one of about a dozen modern cottages erected by the side of the Great Eastern Railway — consistent with speculative terrace development of the period but not specifically Warner.

Do NOT state this as a Warner flat in the book. State it as consistent with but not confirmed as Warner development.

### Internal layout — current best understanding
The house was a first-floor flat in a two-storey terrace. Ground floor: Clarke's quarters (see caveat below) and back door/garden access. First floor: Gouldstone accommodation.

The Gouldstones occupied: a back bedroom, a kitchen (where the fire guard was and the boys played), and a scullery/wash-house (where the cistern was). The trial testimony refers to two kitchens and a back room. The Liverpool Mercury refers to two back rooms on the first floor. These may not be contradictory — one back room functioned as kitchen, the other as wash-house/scullery.

The Ex-Warner Project oral histories confirm: first floor flats had a small balcony with external metal stairs descending to the garden. Ada went downstairs — meaning she descended these external stairs. Clarke was in the garden at the bottom. When Ada reached the bottom she heard the screams from the first floor above her.

Ada and her husband lodged in the rooms above the Gouldstones — confirmed by the coroner deposition (*lodged in the rooms above*). This places Ada on the second floor, not the first floor as one trial testimony reading suggested. The two-storey building per the IPN illustration means Ada's rooms were at the top of the house.

### Clarke caveat
Clarke is described as the landlady. We have assumed she lived on the ground floor of the same building. This is NOT confirmed. She was in the garden when the screams were heard. A landlady could be in her tenant's garden without living in the building. Clarke's place of residence is unconfirmed. Do not state she lived on the ground floor without finding a source that says so.

### The cistern
In the scullery/wash-house, off the kitchen. Folkard: top six feet from the floor, ten inches from the ceiling, fourteen inches of water. Ran the full length of the room. Consistent with the copper/cistern described in Ex-Warner Project oral histories as the standard feature of the original sculleries — used for heating water and doing laundry.

## THE SEQUENCE OF EVENTS — REVISED AND CONFIRMED

The Guardian Journal Nottingham (9 August 1883 — earliest press account, published the morning after) gives the clearest account of the sequence and should be weighted heavily.

Key passage: *just before going she heard Gouldstone ask for the boy Frederick, and noticed that the lad seemed pleased at this parental recognition. Shortly afterwards she was surprised at seeing the cistern overflowing, and going to it she was horrified at discovering the dead bodies of three of the children in it. At the same time she heard screams proceeding from the room where Mrs. Gouldstone was recovering after her confinement.*

**The boys were drowned first. The twins were attacked second.**

This is confirmed by Ada's own trial testimony: *when I was on the stairs going down I heard the kitchen door open and I heard the prisoner go into the bedroom.* She heard William go from the kitchen to the bedroom while she was still on the stairs. The boys were already in the cistern before she reached the bottom.

**William told Ada to leave the house** — not just the room. The Guardian Journal says: *He told her he wanted her to leave the house whilst he had a chat with his wife concerning the money that was to be paid to the doctor.* Ada left. On the stairs she heard the kitchen door, then William going to the bedroom. At the bottom she saw or heard the cistern overflowing and found the boys. Simultaneously she heard Elizabeth screaming.

**The cistern overflowed.** Three children drowned in it caused it to overflow. This is the physical evidence that drew Ada's attention — she saw the overflow before or simultaneously with hearing the screams.

**Full revised sequence:**

1. Ada goes downstairs, William having asked her to leave the house.
2. On the stairs, she hears the kitchen door open. William goes to the kitchen.
3. William drowns Charles, Herbert, and Frederick in the cistern. Hard work with the two biggest. Frederick quickly.
4. William goes from the kitchen to the bedroom. Ada, still on the stairs or at the bottom, hears this movement.
5. William attacks the twins with a carpenter's hammer. They are at Elizabeth's breast.
6. William locks the bedroom door from the inside.
7. Elizabeth screams.
8. Ada, at the bottom of the external stairs, sees the cistern overflowing and finds the three boys. Simultaneously hears the screams.
9. Ada runs back up. Tries the bedroom door. Cannot force it. Elizabeth screaming through it: *Open the door. He is murdering my children.*
10. Clarke arrives from the garden. Both women at the door.
11. William opens the door. Says: *You can come in, it is all done.*
12. Ada goes to the twins. William goes back to the kitchen — to retrieve the boys from the cistern.
13. Ada follows William to the kitchen and meets him carrying Frederick from the scullery, apparently dead. She is horrified and runs back to the bedroom.
14. Ada stays with Elizabeth for ten minutes.
15. When she returns, all three boys are laid on the floor in a row. All dead from drowning.
16. Cheeseman arrives at approximately 5:40pm.

**William's cell statement at nine o'clock:** *I wish I had killed the little ones out of the way. I don't know whether I hit them once or twice.* This refers to the twins, not the boys. He regrets that he attacked them in the bedroom in front of Elizabeth — he wished he had done it away from her. The uncertainty about how many times he struck them reflects the fog of a violent act done quickly in a confined space with his wife present, not uncertainty about the outcome.

**The charge:** Folkard charged William that evening with murdering Charles, Herbert and Frederick and *attempting* to murder the two infants. The twins were still alive. He upgraded the charge to five murders the following morning when told the twins had died overnight. William's response: *Yes, Sir. The other two are dead then.*

## LATE SESSION ADDITIONS — WALTHAMSTOW RESEARCH

### Key findings from Walthamstow archive and local history sources

**Warner Estate — definitively NOT the builder of the murder house.**
The Warner Estate Company was registered 1891. The murder house predates it by nearly a decade. Warner's residential development centred on Leucha Road and the Clock House estate west of St James's Street. His commercial development was the shopping terraces on St James Street and High Street. Courtenay Place, north of the station, is in neither zone. The house was built by an unknown speculative builder in the early 1880s as part of the rapid development that followed the opening of St James Street station in 1871.

**Named local builders active in the area in the 1880s:**
- Edward Fuller, family builders based in Beulah Road E17, active from the 1860s (Biography File at Vestry House)
- Joseph Amey, building business at 102 Beulah Road, active in the 1880s
Either could have built the Courtenay Place cottages. Worth citing in the Vestry House enquiry.

**Queens Road address discrepancy — now more serious.**
The lost streets database confirms Queen's Road East ran from Hoe Street to the Cemetery, and Queen's Road West from Lennox Road to Markhouse Road — both south of the railway. The murder house was north of the railway. The 1935 coroner's report address of 2 Helen Cottages, Queens Road is almost certainly an error. One possible explanation: the Walthamstow Coroner's Court is itself on Queen's Road. Whoever compiled the 1935 document may have accidentally recorded the court's own address rather than the prisoner's address.

**Demolition date of Courtenay Place — unknown.**
NLS map (1890s) shows terrace standing. Satellite image shows bus depot. No demolition date found. Possible causes: bomb damage in Second World War (Walthamstow was heavily hit); postwar redevelopment; bus depot expansion. Worth asking Vestry House and TfL/London Transport heritage archives.

**William Gouldstone has a biography file at Vestry House Museum.**
Listed in the published Biography File index as: "Gouldstone, William — Convicted of killing his 5 children 1883." This is the single most important archival finding of this session. The file may contain press cuttings, rate book entries, photographs, neighbourhood accounts, or other material not available online.

**Frederick W. Vernham (1851-1908), Vicar of St James, Walthamstow, has a biography file at Vestry House.**
The BMJ of 29 September 1883 refers to "the clear statement of the Vicar of the parish in which he lived." Vernham was almost certainly that vicar. His file may contain the statement itself or a reference to it.

## LATE SESSION — WEB RESEARCH FINDS

### Clarke identity resolved
Emma Clarke was the wife of James Clarke, a carpenter. James Clarke HIRED the house at 8 Courtenay Place — he was the tenant, not the owner. The Gouldstones sublet from the Clarkes. Both families rented from the building's actual landlord, who is unknown. Clarke was not a property-owning landlady — she was the head tenant who sublet rooms to the Gouldstones.

Source: Coroner's inquest as reported in the IPN, 18 August 1883.

### The Tuke and Savage pamphlet — confirmed as a separately published work
Daniel Hack Tuke and George Henry Savage co-authored: *The Cases of Gouldstone & Cole*. Lewes: H.W. Wolff, 1883.

This is a separately published pamphlet dealing with both the Gouldstone and Cole murders together. It is cited repeatedly in Victorian and later legal/medical literature. Tuke was editor of the Journal of Mental Science — Savage's superior. This pamphlet is distinct from Savage's articles in the Journal of Mental Science.

### Trial of Lunatics Act 1883 — context
Passed in 1883, the same year as the murders and trial. Permitted a verdict of guilty but insane, with committal to Broadmoor at Her Majesty's Pleasure. William was one of the first major cases tried in its shadow. The public debate around cases like his helped drive the legislative reform.

### The Falkirk Ironworks, Upper Thames Street — identified
The Falkirk Ironworks was the Scottish ironworks company (founded Falkirk 1810) with a London depot/showroom/office at Upper Thames Street. The Kennard family were the proprietors. The London connection via Upper Thames Street is confirmed from 1830 onwards. William had worked at the London office for five years, since approximately 1878.

His foreman William Graves had known William since birth — suggesting the Gouldstone family had a pre-existing connection to the firm, possibly through his father's generation.

Howard John Kinnaird was one of the partners — this is almost certainly Howard John Kennard (the Spectator's account, from the Old Bailey, gives the name as Kinnaird but this is likely a transcription error for Kennard, the family who ran the firm).

### House layout — new detail from IPN coroner account
The IPN coroner account gives Ada's testimony: "Just as Mrs Gouldstone was getting into bed again her husband came along the passage and looked into the room."

And: "she saw the prisoner coming in at the kitchen door from the washhouse with the boy Frederick in his arms."

CONFIRMED LAYOUT:
- Bedroom → PASSAGE → Kitchen → Kitchen door → Washhouse/scullery
- The passage connecting bedroom to kitchen is now confirmed in testimony
- The kitchen has a separate door to the washhouse/scullery — not open plan
- This is consistent with the Old Bailey testimony: "I went through the wash-house to the other kitchen on the same floor"
