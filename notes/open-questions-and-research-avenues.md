## SECTION 1: GENUINE UNRESOLVED CONTRADICTIONS

### 1.1 Address of the murder scene
Two confirmed addresses from separate documents:
- "8 Courtenay Place, St James's Street, Walthamstow" — Ada Hamilton's witness statement and all 1883 press. USE THIS.
- "2 Helen Cottages, Queens Road, West Walthamstow" — coroner's report 1935, compiled 52 years after the fact.

**Updated analysis (28 June 2026):** The Queens Road address is almost certainly an error. The lost streets database and VCH confirm that Queen's Road East and Queen's Road West were both south of the railway line. The murder house was north of the railway beside St James Street station. Furthermore, the Walthamstow Coroner's Court is itself on Queen's Road — whoever compiled the 1935 document may have recorded the court's own address rather than the prisoner's address. The current Courtenay Place E17 dates from 1893 — a different street from the 1883 address.

**Action:** Vestry House Museum rate books and W28 St James's Street buildings file. Resolve before Part Two is drafted.

---

### 1.2 Penny Illustrated — mother's death
RESOLVED. The mention of William's mother found dead was a conflation with a subsequent unrelated article about a Mrs Pole. Discard entirely. Emily Willett Gouldstone appears alive in the 1901 and 1911 census in Great Sampford.

---

### 1.3 Clarke — tenant or landlord?
Emma Clarke is described in press reports as "the landlady." Ada Hamilton's coroner deposition says "the landlady of the house who was in the garden." But the coroner's inquest (IPN, 18 August 1883) identifies her as "Emma Clarke, wife of James Clarke, a carpenter, of 8 Courtenay Place" — and James Clarke HIRED the house, i.e. he was the head tenant, not the owner. Both families rented. Clarke was a tenant-landlady subletting to the Gouldstones, not the building's owner.

**Action:** Rate books for Courtenay Place 1880-1900 (Vestry House Museum) will identify the actual building owner.

---

### 1.4 Was the house a Warner Estate property?
NOT CONFIRMED and now considered unlikely. The Warner Estate Company was registered 1891 — eight years after the murders. The Courtenay Place cottages were built in the early 1880s, before the formal Warner operation began. Warner's residential development was centred on Leucha Road and the Clock House estate west of St James's Street. The murder house was north of the station, outside Warner's known development zone.

The house was almost certainly built by a local speculative builder — possibly Edward Fuller (Beulah Road E17, active from 1860s) or Joseph Amey (102 Beulah Road, active 1880s), both confirmed as local builders in the Vestry House biography files.

**Do NOT describe the house as a Warner flat in the book.**

### 2.6 Did William go to a specific location to drown himself on 8 August?
The BMJ Medical News of 6 October 1883 states: "It is believed that he went out with the intention of drowning himself on the very day that he murdered his children." He came home having failed. The cord in his pocket was almost certainly brought for himself, not the children. Where did he go? The river Lea was nearby. 

**Action:** Home Office papers may clarify. Cannot be resolved from press sources alone.

### 2.7 The insane aunt and the suicidal great-uncle — unidentified
The BMJ Medical News of 6 October 1883 states: "His mother and aunt were both insane and his great-uncle committed suicide." We know the mother (Emily Willett Gouldstone, née Housden) had multiple episodes of severe depression and made multiple suicide attempts. The aunt and great-uncle are unidentified.

**Action:** Essex parish records for the Gouldstone family, Great Sampford. Essex Record Office. Home Office papers may also identify them.

---

### 2.8 Elizabeth said she wished the children were dead — primary source needed
The BMJ of 22 September 1883 states: "a thoughtless exclamation of his wife opened up to him a more excellent way. She said she wished the children were dead." This is presented as contributing to William's decision. However The Times of 11 August 1883 reports Emma Clarke saying Elizabeth "had said she wished the children were dead when they worried her" — and Clarke added she did not think Elizabeth was in earnest.

The BMJ version elevates this to a causal factor. The Clarke corroboration from The Times adds some weight. But the BMJ may be reading more significance into Clarke's evidence than it can bear. Use with caution. Do not present in book as a causal factor without flagging that it is a BMJ inference from Clarke's testimony.

### 2.10 Demolition date of Courtenay Place — unknown
The NLS OS map from the 1890s shows the terrace at Courtenay Place standing. The site is now a bus depot. No demolition date has been found. Possible causes: wartime bomb damage, postwar redevelopment, bus depot expansion.

**Action:** TfL / London Transport heritage archives — when was the bus depot at St James Street built? Vestry House Museum W28 buildings file may contain demolition date. London Borough of Waltham Forest planning archives if post-1947.

## SECTION 5: ITEMS REQUIRING VERIFICATION

1. **The James Barnes letter** in IPN 18 Aug 1883 — uncertain whether this relates to the Gouldstone case or the Cole copycat case. Requires verification.

2. **Albert's enlistment date vs William Madams death date** — Albert enlisted 19 June 1901; Madams died 23 November 1901. Verify exact enlistment date from naval record.

3. **Blacksmith vs whitesmith** — William listed as blacksmith on 1881 census and Newgate Calendars; whitesmith on trial documents and Graves testimony. Graves confirms he "made him a whitesmith." The census/Newgate error was an enumerator's simplification. Use whitesmith throughout.

4. **Howard John Kinnaird vs Kennard** — the Old Bailey transcript gives the Falkirk Ironworks partner as "Kinnaird" but this is likely a transcription error for Kennard, the family who ran the firm from 1830. Verify against Falkirk Ironworks records.
