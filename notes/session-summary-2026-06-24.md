### Key Savage quotes recovered:

- "A morbid development, not a devilish afflatus."
- To The Times, 15 September 1883: William was "seized with fear of impending ruin to himself and family, and kills them to send them to heaven" — an insane act. "I trust this prisoner will not be allowed to be hanged."
- To Daily Telegraph, 17 September 1883: Poland's cross-examination "gave me no opportunity of representing my own opinion on the man's sanity. It was melancholia out of relation to its causes and its end. The whole thing was — as is general in mental disorder — a morbid development, not a devilish afflatus."

### Dr Orange and Dr Clarke
Two alienists commissioned by Home Secretary Sir William Harcourt to examine William after conviction. Their report finding him insane secured the reprieve. The examination may be in the Broadmoor files.

### Foreman of the jury
Published a card in the Daily Telegraph explaining that Judge Day told them: if a person was proved to be of sound mind up to the time of the act, knew the nature of the act and its penalty, and appeared of sound mind afterwards, they were bound to find them not insane. The jury had almost no room.

- Parents: Thomas Stock (Agricultural Labourer, born 1821) and Jemima Ansell née Suckling (prior marriage Suckling); married 16 June 1854, both previously widowed

- October 1873: mother Jemima died ("Neuralgia 6 weeks, congestion of brain 3 weeks")
- November 1873: father Thomas died ("Bronchitis Chronic 12 months") — still listed as Ag. Lab. at age 70
- Both parents dead within a month of each other. Elizabeth eighteen, entirely alone, no siblings, no family home.
- 1879: married William Gouldstone at St Luke's, Hackney. Five years after her parents died.

## William Gouldstone — Background Established

- Born ~1856-57, Great Sampford, Essex. Son of Thomas Gouldstone (blacksmith, born 1821, whole life in Great Sampford, died 1899) and Emily Willett (born 1834, Wimbish — three miles from Great Sampford)
- William's mother Emily suffered sustained severe depression; made multiple suicide attempts; Thomas sat up with her all night hiding the knives
- William's maternal aunt: chronic lunatic with suicidal tendencies and persistent delusions of ruin
- Second cousin died in a lunatic asylum; several other paternal relatives with mental illness (judge dismissed second cousins as legally irrelevant)
- William's siblings: Emily (1852), Robert (1854), Thomas (1859), Flora (1864), Hugh C (1868), Mabel Frances (1874) — seven children total
- William came to London, found work at Falkirk Ironworks, Upper Thames Street, through William Graves (foreman, born Radwinter Essex — same cluster of north Essex parishes as Great Sampford and Wethersfield)
- William Graves had known William since he was born — Essex connection predates London
- 1879: William and Elizabeth married at St Luke's, Hackney, both living at 88 Frampton Park Road (Graves's house). William listed as Iron Worker on marriage certificate. Father James Goldstone (smith) witnessed the wedding.
- 1879-~1882: William and Elizabeth lodged with Graves. Three eldest children born in Graves's house.
- Graves promoted William from blacksmith to whitesmith. Wage: 25 shillings a week.
- Bennett Gouldstone (William's brother) also lived at 88 Frampton Park Road as boarder in 1881, aged ~19, also working at Falkirk Ironworks
- ~1882: William and Elizabeth moved to 8 Courtenay Place, St James's Street, Walthamstow — their first home of their own, two kitchens and one back room
- Ada Hamilton and husband took front room on same floor ~10 weeks before the murders

### William's deterioration (testimony):
- Bennett: "about four months ago he said to me that he should like to throw himself down the lift hole — he was suffering very much from rupture — he said he should like to kill himself out of his misery — he complained of pain about his head, and rupture at the time — he used to wander about as if something were wrong in him, and be very strange."
- Bennett in cross-examination: William did his work regularly but suffered greatly from rupture. Bennett did not know where William was on the Saturday of the beanfeast, or on the Monday, Tuesday, or Wednesday before the murders — he was not at work.
- Graves: "he had many peculiar little ways, but being foreman I did not mix up with the men only so far as my duty compelled me"
- Workmates heard him ask to be knocked on the head with a hammer; say he would throw himself down the lift shaft or in front of a train
- Visions and hallucinations reported: hearing the voice of God, seeing heaven open
- To Dr Savage: "I know it was only dreaming" — partial insight, describes psychotic episode as a dream
- Foreman noticed him deteriorating the day after the twins were born

### The beanfeast sequence:
- Friday 1 August: twins born
- Friday 3 August: beanfeast. Graves paid William his wages plus customary 10 shilling gift. William went "where he thought proper."
- Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday: William absent from work, whereabouts unknown even to his brother
- Wednesday 8 August, 5:15pm: William came home two hours early

### The rupture:
- Chronic hernia causing constant worsening pain — no effective treatment available to a working man in 1883
- Suicidal ideation rooted in chronic physical pain, in a man with hereditary depression on both sides

## The Silence

Albert never spoke about his family or origins. His daughters knew almost nothing.
Mick Ferguson never spoke about Albert or his own father George to his children.
The silence reproduced itself across generations without anyone deciding to reproduce it.
The secret had stopped being a lie and become the family's understanding of itself.

Family versions of the story collected:
- **Lil's version (via Dot to Linda):** Born in France, son of a French maid and a nobleman, hushed up
- **Sue's version:** German, based on Goldstone/Goldstein similarity and "the Goldstone nose"
- **Steve's version:** Uncovered German birthplace on naval record
- **Rene's verdict:** "Absolute rubbish"
- **Rik's discovery:** The blank space on the birth certificate. The genealogy research. The Germany lie unravelling. The causal chain understood.

## Additional Findings — Media Coverage Read This Session

### The Lancet Letter (Savage, 14 September 1883)
**Source:** William Gouldstone Lancet letter.pdf — fully transcribed, typeset text readable.
Written from Bethlem Hospital on the day of sentencing.

Key content:

**Hallucinations confirmed in Savage's own words:**
"He told me himself, but this was not brought out in evidence, that he had very bad dreams and had seen heaven opened and had heard the voice of God, but, he added, he knew it was only dreaming."
This was never put before the jury. The most significant evidence of psychosis was excluded because counsel were not permitted to ask Savage his opinion directly.

**Judge Day's conduct:**
"The judge ridiculed the notion of bringing in second cousins, and even threw doubt upon my bona fides when I said that marked insanity on one side, and taint, though distant, on the other would increase the risk of insanity in the offspring."
Judge Day publicly questioned Savage's good faith from the bench.

**The rope:**
"He also told the policeman he was now ready for the rope" — William told a policeman he was ready to hang. The judge used this as evidence he understood the consequences and therefore knew the act was criminal. This detail does not appear clearly in the trial transcript versions we have.

**Savage's summary of William:**
"The prisoner is just what one would have expected from his history; a feeble minded, rather weakly, good man, who did his mechanical work well, and went home to his family. Anxiety about his increasing family at once developed the latent weakness, and he feared ruin, just as so many lunatics do, and took an insane way to get out of the trouble."

**Closing line:**
"If this man is hanged, I think it will be a scandal to law."
Written the day of sentencing, before the reprieve.

This letter is the spine of Part Three.

### BMJ Commutation Piece (date unclear — after conviction, before reprieve)
**Source:** Communtation in newspaper.png
