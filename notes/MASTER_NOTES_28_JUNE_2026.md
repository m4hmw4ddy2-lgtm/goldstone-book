## PART ONE: THE BOOK

### The hook
On the evening of 8 August 1883, a whitesmith named William Gouldstone came home early from work in London. His wife Elizabeth had just given birth to twins. He murdered all five of his children and told the arresting officer he was a happy man. He was wrong. Because of what he did that evening, one hundred and forty years later, a man named Richard Ferguson exists.

### Structure — confirmed
Prologue + 12 chapters + Coda, organised into six parts:

- **Prologue:** 8 August 1883, real time through Ada Hamilton's testimony. Ends on locked door.
- **Part One: Before** — Essex origins. William and Elizabeth's backgrounds. Great Sampford and Wethersfield. The migration to London. The deterioration.
- **Part Two: The Evening** — return to the prologue and complete it. The cistern. Charles. The locked door. The hammer. What Elizabeth said when the door opened.
- **Part Three: The Reckoning** — inquest, trial, medical debate, reprieve, Broadmoor, copycat murder.
- **Part Four: Elizabeth** — survival, the four missing years, Lambeth, Albert's birth, Whitstable, William Madams.
- **Part Five: Albert** — the Germany lie, the Navy, the wars, sixty-one years of fabrication.
- **Part Six: The Researcher** — the blank space on the birth certificate, the thread, the causal chain understood.
- **Coda** — William Gouldstone is not Rik's ancestor. He is the reason Rik exists. The Broadmoor files open in 2036.

### The central argument
If William Gouldstone does not murder his five children on 8 August 1883, Elizabeth remains in a functioning marriage. She does not disappear to Lambeth. She does not meet William Madams, or if she meets him she does not form a relationship with him. Albert is never born. Vera Georgina Goldstone is never born. Michael George Ferguson is never born. Rik is never born.

William Gouldstone is not Rik's ancestor. He is the reason Rik exists.

## PART TWO: THE PROLOGUE — FINAL TEXT

On the evening of Wednesday the 8th of August 1883, William Gouldstone came home from work two hours early. He had not been at work.

Elizabeth Gouldstone, already a mother to three boys, had given birth to twins seven days earlier. She was lying in the back bedroom of 8 Courtenay Place, St James's Street, Walthamstow, while Ada Hamilton washed the babies at her bedside. Ada and her husband had been lodging in the rooms above for ten weeks already and she had been engaged as nurse for Elizabeth's confinement. The three older boys, Charles, Herbert, and Frederick, were in the kitchen.

Unexpectedly, Ada heard someone enter the house. She put down the baby and went to see who was there.

William was standing by the dresser. He was twenty-six years old, and his usual return from the ironworks wasn't until half past seven. It was a quarter past five.

Ada said: "You did frighten me. I wondered who it was."

He smiled but made no reply. He had been like this since the twins were born, strange and withdrawn, and she had learned not to press him. She went back to the bedroom, finished washing the first twin and laid him next to his mother and brother.

When she returned to the kitchen, William had settled himself on the fire guard. The three boys were playing on the floor around him and he was watching them, saying nothing. He had a length of cord in his fingers, turning it slowly. She asked whether he had had his tea yet, and he replied only, "No." She told him it was ready but he made no reply to that at all, so she went back to see to Elizabeth and the babies.

Once she had finished washing the second twin, as she had done all afternoon, she went back through the kitchen toward the scullery. The boys were still playing. William was still on the fire guard, still watching them, the cord still in his hands. In the scullery, she noticed that the perambulator had been moved from its place beside the cistern and a chair put there instead. The cistern sat six feet from the floor. It held fourteen inches of water.

She turned to ask who had moved the chair. William heard her, but it was three-year-old Charles who answered: "My daddy put it there."

Back in the bedroom, Ada helped Elizabeth out of bed so it could be remade, then helped her back in. When William appeared in the doorway she told him he could come in, everyone was decent, and he mumbled something she could not make out before going back to the kitchen. She suggested to Elizabeth that she might leave them alone for a few minutes, to give William the chance to speak to his wife privately. Elizabeth agreed.

Downstairs, just as Ada reached the back door, she heard screams of "Murder!" from above.

She ran back up and tried the bedroom door, but it was locked from the inside. She tried to force it and could not. Elizabeth was screaming through it: "Open the door. He is murdering my children."

Ada cried out for help.

*I wasn't looking for William Gouldstone. I was looking for someone else entirely. But this is the story I found. A murder that shocked the nation and that created my family.*

### Prologue decisions — locked
- Opens on two sentences establishing premeditation. "He had not been at work" is the first revelation.
- Ends on the locked door. Everything behind it belongs to Part Two.
- Cistern measurements at the moment Ada notices the chair — reader does the arithmetic.
- Free indirect style for Ada's unease.
- "still" construction three times in the scullery paragraph.
- Four closing sentences in Rik's own voice.
- Em-dashes forbidden. None present.
- Twins are male throughout.

## PART THREE: THE CRIME — CONFIRMED FACTS

### The people in the house, 8 August 1883
- **William Gouldstone** (26), whitesmith, husband and father
- **Elizabeth Gouldstone** (28), seven days post-partum with twins
- **Charles Thomas Gouldstone** (3½), eldest boy
- **Herbert Ernest Gouldstone** (2½), second boy
- **Frederick Gouldstone** (16 months), third boy
- **Twin boys** (7 days old), unnamed in the record
- **Ada Hamilton**, monthly nurse, lodging on second floor with husband William Hamilton (jeweller)
- **Emma Clarke**, wife of James Clarke (carpenter), head tenant of the house — in the garden

### The house — confirmed
**Address:** 8 Courtenay Place, St James's Street, Walthamstow. Use this throughout.

**2 Helen Cottages, Queens Road** (1935 coroner's report) is almost certainly an error. Queens Road is south of the railway; the murder house was north of it. The Walthamstow Coroner's Court is itself on Queens Road — the 1935 compiler may have recorded the court's address rather than the prisoner's.

**What it was:** One of about a dozen modern cottages erected beside the Great Eastern Railway, built speculatively in the early 1880s. NOT a Warner Estate property — Warner Estate Company not registered until 1891. Built by unknown speculative builder, possibly Edward Fuller or Joseph Amey (both confirmed local builders in Vestry House biography files).

**What stands there now:** Bus depot. Demolished at unknown date (post-1890s OS map, pre-2014 walking tour blog).

**Internal layout — confirmed from testimony:**
- Ground floor: Clarke territory (residence unconfirmed — she was in garden)
- First floor: Gouldstone accommodation
  - Back bedroom (Elizabeth, twins, Ada washing babies)
  - Passage connecting bedroom to kitchen (confirmed: "her husband came along the passage and looked into the room")
  - Kitchen (fire guard, where boys played, where William sat)
  - Kitchen door to washhouse/scullery (confirmed: "saw the prisoner coming in at the kitchen door from the washhouse")
  - Washhouse/scullery (cistern, perambulator, chair placed under cistern)
  - Small balcony/landing with external metal stairs to garden
- Second floor: Ada and William Hamilton (front room — confirmed by coroner deposition)

**The cistern:** In the washhouse/scullery. Six feet from the floor. Ten inches from the ceiling. Fourteen inches of water. Ran the full length of the room.

### The sequence — confirmed
**Boys drowned first. Twins attacked second.** Confirmed by three independent sources: Guardian Journal Nottingham (9 August), Ada Hamilton's staircase testimony, BMJ 22 September 1883.

1. William comes home at 5:15pm. Two hours early. Had not been at work.
2. Ada in bedroom washing second twin.
3. William in kitchen with the boys, cord in his fingers, watching them.
4. Ada goes to scullery, notices perambulator moved, chair under cistern. Charles: "My daddy put it there."
5. Ada back to bedroom. Helps Elizabeth remake bed. William appears in doorway, mumbles, returns to kitchen.
6. William asks Ada to leave the house — wants to speak to Elizabeth about doctor's fees.
7. Ada goes down the external stairs toward the garden.
8. On the stairs she hears the kitchen door open. William goes to the kitchen.
9. William drowns Charles, Herbert, and Frederick in the cistern. Hard work with the two biggest. Frederick quickly. Cistern overflows.
10. William goes from kitchen to bedroom. Locks the door. Attacks the twins with a carpenter's hammer while they are at Elizabeth's breast.
11. Elizabeth screams "Murder!"
12. Ada, at the bottom of the external stairs, sees or hears the cistern overflowing. Simultaneously hears the screams.
13. Ada runs back up. Tries the bedroom door. Cannot force it. Elizabeth screams through it: "Open the door. He is murdering my children."
14. Clarke arrives from the garden.
15. William opens the door. "You can come in, it is all done."
16. Ada goes to the twins. William returns to the kitchen to retrieve the boys.
17. Ada follows William to kitchen, meets him carrying Frederick from the scullery, apparently dead. Runs back to bedroom in horror.
18. Ada stays with Elizabeth ten minutes.
19. When she returns, all three boys are in a row on the floor. All dead from drowning.
20. Cheeseman arrives ~5:40pm, finds William stooped over the boys.
21. William says: "I did it like a man."

### William's words — in sequence
- To Ada when she forced open the door: "No, I am a happy man."
- To Ada: "I have killed my children. I am a happy man now. Your other three children are in the cistern."
- To Elizabeth: "I have killed all your children; now you will be single again, and I shall be hanged."
- To Cheeseman on walk to station: "When I took my money last week I thought of buying a revolver to do it with, but I altered my mind, because I thought it would make too much noise. I had a hard job with the two biggest, but the other little one I soon settled him. I thought it was getting too hot to have five children in about three years and a half, so I thought I would put a stop to it."
- At station: "I've done it like a man."
- Police cell, 5:45pm: "I have done it now. I am happy and ready for the rope."
- Police cell, 7:00pm: "There is five of them gone to glory, and it is a good job."
- Police cell, 7:30pm: "I am sorry for my wife and parents, but the children will be better off in heaven, if there is such a place; it is better than leaving them to the mercy of the world."
- Police cell, 9:00pm: "I wish I had killed the little ones out of the way; I don't know whether I hit them once or twice; I have had this preying on my mind for a long time."

**Note on the revolver decision:** The BMJ's reading (22 September 1883) is correct. He avoided the revolver not to avoid detection but because he feared being interrupted before completing the act. This is the reading that belongs in Part Two and Part Three.

**Note on "preying":** The constable corrected this in cross-examination. The word was *preying*, not *playing*.

**Note on the cell statement at 9:00pm:** "I wish I had killed the little ones out of the way" refers to the twins. He wished he had done it away from Elizabeth, not in front of her. He came home intending first to drown himself — the twins were attacked second because he failed to kill himself before coming home.

### William went out that morning intending to drown himself
**Source:** BMJ Medical News, 6 October 1883: "It is believed that he went out with the intention of drowning himself on the very day that he murdered his children."

He failed. He came home with a cord in his pocket. The cord was almost certainly brought for himself, not the children. The chair under the cistern — was it first intended for himself?

This reframes the entire sequence. He came home having failed to die. He came home to his five children.

Source reliability: "It is believed" — not directly attributed. Almost certainly from Dr Orange's examination at Chelmsford or Elizabeth's account to the Home Office. Treat as well-sourced inference pending primary source confirmation in HO 144 papers.

### The days before the murders
- Twins born 1 August 1883.
- From that moment a change was noticed in William's demeanour. He was seen crying on two occasions.
- During the following week he was twice the worse for drink.
- On the Saturday he absented himself from the annual bean-feast of his fellow-workmen.
- The next three days (Sunday, Monday, Tuesday) he wandered about.
- On the Wednesday (8 August) he went out intending to drown himself, failed, came home and murdered the children.
- He was therefore absent from work from at least Saturday through Wednesday — four to five days.
- Elizabeth says he showed signs of madness for twelve months before the murders — onset approximately October 1882, predating the pregnancy.

### William's family history of mental illness
- Mother: multiple episodes of severe depression, multiple suicide attempts. Most recent attempt approximately late July 1883, just after the twins were born.
- Aunt: insane (BMJ 6 October 1883). Which aunt, which side — unidentified.
- Great-uncle: committed suicide (BMJ 6 October 1883). Unidentified.
- Second cousin on the father's side: William Gouldstone (a soldier), taken to Brentford Lunatic Asylum 21 April 1880, confined about 16 months, died there. Had been subject to fits.
- BMJ: "a terrible disease which has scourged his family for three generations."
- William suffered from rupture — physical condition causing pain and low spirits (Bennett Gouldstone, trial testimony).
- William suffered from constant headaches in the days before the murders (BMJ 22 September — unverified, may be inference).

### William's workplace
The Falkirk Ironworks, Upper Thames Street, London. The London depot of the Scottish Falkirk Ironworks, established by the Kennard family from 1830. A large firm with fully 1,000 workmen. William had been there five years, since approximately 1878. His foreman William Graves had known William since birth — the family had a pre-existing connection to the firm.

Partners included Howard John Kinnaird (almost certainly a transcription error for Kennard). Kinnaird confirmed: "The whole feeling of his fellow-workmen is that he is certainly insane."

William's siblings Bennett and Emily also connected to the firm or London.

### The trial — 14 September 1883
- Prosecuted: Poland and Montagu Williams
- Defended: Grain and Elliott
- Expert witness: Dr George Henry Savage (examined William once, for between a quarter and half an hour, the day before trial)
- Judge: Mr Justice Day (applied narrow McNaughton Rules, made light of insanity evidence)
- Jury: fifteen minutes deliberation. Guilty on all five counts.
- Sentence: Death.

Dr Orange of Broadmoor and Dr Gover (HM Inspector of Prisons) sent to examine William at Chelmsford. Both entertained no doubt of his insanity. Orange's report almost certainly caused the reprieve.

**Reprieve:** 3 October 1883. Home Secretary Sir William Harcourt. Committed to Broadmoor at Her Majesty's Pleasure.

William received the news "with deep gratitude."

**Broadmoor:** Admitted 15 October 1883. William Orange was Medical Superintendent 1870-1886. William was described as "an ideal patient. He worked in the various wards." Died 19 January 1935. Cause of death: hypostatic pneumonia following myocardial degeneration. 52 years confined. Age 78 (the Evening Standard said 80 — likely an approximation).

**Broadmoor files:** Sealed until 2036 (101 years after death). Confirmed to contain family correspondence.

### The copycat murder — James Cole, Thornton Heath
19 August 1883 — eleven days after Gouldstone. Cole had spent the Sunday reading about the Gouldstone case and told his wife he would settle his own children in the same way. Murdered his three-year-old son that same evening. Tried and executed.

The BMJ cited this explicitly: "the execution of a lunatic is generally followed by a copious crop of lunatic homicides." The Tuke and Savage pamphlet (1883) deals with both cases together.

### The Trial of Lunatics Act 1883
Passed the same year as the murders. Permitted a verdict of guilty but insane, with committal to Broadmoor at Her Majesty's Pleasure. William was one of the first major cases tried in its shadow. The public debate around cases like his helped drive the legislative reform.

## PART FOUR: THE KEY PEOPLE

### William Gouldstone
Born ~1857, Great Sampford area, Essex. Whitesmith. Married Elizabeth Stock 15 March 1879, St Luke, Hackney. Father of the five murdered children. Died Broadmoor 19 January 1935.

### Elizabeth Stock
Born 1855, Wethersfield, Essex. William's wife. Survived the murders. Was lying in bed seven days post-partum when her husband locked the bedroom door with her and the dying twins inside.

Her words in sequence:
- Through locked door: "Come in; he is murdering my children." / "Open the door. He is murdering my children." / "Good God! he is murdering my children." (variant accounts)
- To William when police brought him back: "Oh you wicked man, what have you done?"
- Then: "Have you any money?"
- Public statement, September 1883: "I have no doubt he did not know and could not help what he did. He was always most affectionate to me and the children, and I cannot think that he should suffer a shameful death for what would be to him, if he only understood it, the most awful distress."

She could not divorce William. He was alive in Broadmoor. Insanity was not straightforward grounds for divorce in Victorian England. She was legally Mrs Gouldstone until his death in 1935.

She disappears from the record from August 1883 until June 1887, when Albert was born at 2 John Street, Hercules Buildings, Lambeth.

Died July 1906, Blean, Kent.

### Ada Hamilton
Monthly nurse. Wife of William Hamilton, jeweller. Lodged in the second floor rooms above the Gouldstones for ten weeks before the murders. Her testimony is the spine of the Prologue and Part Two.

### Emma Clarke
Wife of James Clarke, carpenter. James Clarke hired (rented) the house at 8 Courtenay Place and sublet to the Gouldstones. Clarke was the head tenant, not the building's owner. Both families rented from an unknown actual landlord. Emma was in the garden hanging out clothes when the murders occurred.

### Dr George Henry Savage
Physician-Superintendent, Bethlem Hospital. Examined William once, for between a quarter and half an hour, the day before trial. Gave hedged testimony — found his conversation rational, would not certify him insane, but thought his mind was probably unsound at the time of the act. The BMJ criticised him for not going far enough.

Later: co-editor of the Journal of Mental Science, President of the Medico-Psychological Association, knighted 1912. Treated Virginia Woolf for a decade. Woolf satirised him as Sir William Bradshaw in *Mrs Dalloway*.

### Dr William Orange
Medical Superintendent, Broadmoor, 1870-1886. Sent to Chelmsford to examine William after the death sentence. Entertained no doubt of his insanity. His report almost certainly caused the reprieve. Responsible for William for the first three years of his Broadmoor detention.

### Reverend Frederick W. Vernham (1851-1908)
Vicar of St James, Walthamstow. Almost certainly the vicar whose statement the BMJ described as helping secure the reprieve ("the clear statement of the Vicar of the parish in which he lived," BMJ 29 September 1883). Has a biography file at Vestry House Museum.

### Albert Jennet Goldstone (1887-1962)
Elizabeth's son. Almost certainly the biological son of William James Madams of Whitstable, though registered as father unknown at the Lambeth Workhouse Infirmary in June 1887.

The Germany lie: from his naval enlistment in June 1901, Albert claimed to have been born in Germany. Maintained this on every subsequent document for 61 years.

Naval career: served 1901-1919. Second Battle of Heligoland Bight (November 1917). Present at the surrender of the German High Seas Fleet (1918). North Russia intervention (1918-19). After the Navy: ships rigger, later foreman of the ropery at Chatham Dockyard, Chatham Borough Councillor (unconfirmed documentarily).

Rik's great-grandfather: Albert → Vera Georgina Goldstone (b.1921) → George William Ferguson (b.1920) → Michael George Ferguson → Richard David Ferguson.

### William James Madams
Born 1 May 1860, Whitstable, Kent. Almost certainly Albert's biological father. Cohabited with Elizabeth in Whitstable from approximately 1888. No marriage certificate found — Elizabeth could not legally marry while Gouldstone was alive in Broadmoor. Admitted to Chartham Asylum, 4 September 1901. Died 23 November 1901.

## PART FIVE: THE BMJ COVERAGE — SUMMARY

Four articles constitute the BMJ's complete coverage of the case, September-October 1883. All transcribed and in `transcribed/` folder.

**22 September 1883, pp.584-585** — Analysis after death sentence. Boys drowned first, twins second confirmed. Criticises Day and Savage. Calls on Harcourt to review case.

**29 September 1883** — Home Secretary intending to commute. Orange and Gover to examine William. References Elizabeth's narrative (not yet found) and Vicar's statement (not yet found). "The dire compulsion of disease."

**6 October 1883, Medical News** — MOST SIGNIFICANT PIECE. William went out to drown himself the morning of the murders. Mother and aunt insane, great-uncle suicide. Elizabeth says twelve months of signs. Dr Orange entertained no doubt of insanity.

**6 October 1883, pp.682-683** — Editorial response to reprieve. "A terrible disease which has scourged his family for three generations." Dr Guy on lunatic homicides. Youth Ben at Matlock parallel case. Call for reform. "The duplex system." "Gouldstone would have atoned by this time with his life for the crime of being afflicted with a terrible disease."
